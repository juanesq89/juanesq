Korean Dual Nationality and Military Service: The Renunciation Deadline (국적이탈) Explained

By Juan, Attorney at Law 8 min read English

If you're a man who holds Korean citizenship alongside another nationality — often because a parent was Korean when you were born abroad — there is a narrow window to renounce your Korean nationality (국적이탈, gukjeok italse) before Korea's military service obligations attach. Miss it, and Korean law generally treats you as bound to serve, or to remain a Korean national, until the obligation is resolved one way or another.

This is one of the most consequential deadlines that many overseas Korean families never learn about until it's nearly too late — often because the family assumed dual nationality was simply a permanent, low-stakes status. It isn't, for men. Under the Nationality Act (국적법, gukjeokbeop) and the Military Service Act (병역법, byeongyeokbeop), a male dual national's ability to freely renounce Korean citizenship narrows sharply once he approaches military service age, and the consequences of missing the window can follow him for years, including restrictions on entering Korea and on renouncing citizenship later.

→Who This Applies To

  • Men who acquired Korean nationality automatically at birth — typically because one or both parents were Korean nationals — while also holding another country's citizenship, whether by birthright (jus soli) abroad or through a parent's foreign nationality
  • It generally does not apply the same way to women, who face different rules around dual nationality and are not subject to the same military service linkage
  • It applies whether or not the person has ever visited Korea, holds a Korean passport, or considers themselves "really" Korean — the legal nationality exists independently of how connected someone feels to it

→The Renunciation Window Before Military Service Age

A male dual national generally must formally renounce Korean nationality (국적이탈 신고, gukjeok itaseon singo) before a fixed deadline tied to reaching the year he turns 18, after which the ability to freely renounce is cut off and Korean nationality — along with the accompanying military service obligation — generally remains attached unless a narrower exception applies. The exact cutoff date has been the subject of legislative and constitutional litigation in recent years, and getting it wrong by even a matter of weeks can be the difference between a straightforward renunciation and years of complication, so confirming the current precise deadline with an attorney or a Korean consulate before relying on any general description — including this one — is essential.

The renunciation (국적이탈) deadline is not a formality — missing it by even a short window can mean Korean nationality, and the military service obligation attached to it, follows a dual national into adulthood.

→The Overseas Exception

A narrower path exists for dual nationals who have genuinely been raised and based abroad — sometimes called the overseas exception — generally available to those who have not registered a domestic address, have not engaged in for-profit activity in Korea beyond limited permitted periods, and can show their life has actually been centered outside Korea since birth. This exception can allow renunciation later than the general deadline, but it comes with its own documentation burden, and NTS and immigration records of time spent in Korea can undermine an application if the person spent more time in the country than they realized would count against them.

→The 2022 Special Permission Process for Missed Deadlines

Following a 2020 Constitutional Court ruling that found the rigid, no-exceptions version of the deadline unconstitutional as applied to people who missed it for reasons outside their control — commonly, a parent simply never filing the paperwork on the child's behalf — the Nationality Act was amended to add a special permission process (국적이탈 허가, gukjeok italse heoga). This allows someone who missed the original deadline to apply to a nationality review committee for permission to renounce anyway, but it is discretionary, requires a legitimate justification for the delay, and generally requires that the applicant not have taken advantage of Korean nationality — such as entering on a Korean passport or engaging in activities reserved for Korean nationals — after turning 18. It is a real path, but not a guaranteed one, and the application itself requires careful preparation.

→Consequences of Missing the Deadline With No Exception Available

  • Continued Korean nationality — the person generally remains a Korean national on paper, with the accompanying military service obligation, regardless of how the other citizenship treats them
  • Overseas travel permission requirements — Korean men of military service age living abroad without having resolved their status can be required to obtain permission (국외여행허가) for extended time abroad, and failing to do so can create complications on a later trip to Korea
  • Being called up if entering Korea — a dual national who enters Korea, especially to reside or work rather than briefly visit, can be treated as subject to enlistment procedures depending on age and circumstances
  • A later, harder path to renunciation — nationality typically cannot simply be given up afterward without either completing or being formally exempted from military service, or successfully obtaining the special permission described above

→How to Renounce Korean Nationality (국적이탈 신고)

Where a dual national is still within an eligible window, renunciation is filed as a nationality renunciation report (국적이탈 신고) at a Korean consulate or embassy abroad, or with the relevant domestic authority if filing from Korea. Typical supporting documents include proof of the other nationality, a family relation certificate or equivalent showing the Korean parent, and proof of residence establishing the person's actual base of living. Processing can take months, so this is not something to begin at the last minute relative to any applicable deadline.

→What to Do If You or Your Child May Be a Korean Dual National

  • Confirm whether Korean nationality was actually acquired at birth — this depends on the parents' nationality and status at the time, not just ancestry in general
  • If a renunciation deadline is approaching, get precise current guidance well before it — the applicable dates have changed through legislation and court rulings and general online information is frequently outdated
  • If the family has genuinely been based overseas since birth, evaluate whether the overseas exception applies before assuming the general deadline is the only option
  • If a deadline was already missed, don't assume the door is permanently closed — the special permission process (국적이탈 허가) added in 2022 may still provide a path
  • Before any trip to Korea as an adult male dual national with unresolved status, get advice on overseas travel permission (국외여행허가) requirements rather than assuming a short visit carries no risk

Korean dual nationality (이중국적) is a status with real, time-sensitive legal consequences for military-age men — the cost of treating it as a formality rather than a deadline can be measured in years, not paperwork.
