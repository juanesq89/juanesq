Voice Phishing (보이스피싱) in Korea: What Victims — and Accused Accomplices — Need to Know

By Juan, Attorney at Law 8 min read English

A caller claiming to be from the prosecutor's office, a bank, or a delivery company talks someone into wiring money or handing over a bank card "for verification." That's voice phishing (보이스피싱, boiseu pisying) — and in Korea it produces two very different kinds of foreign client: the person who lost money, and the person who unknowingly became a suspect by helping move it.

Voice phishing (보이스피싱) is organized financial fraud conducted by phone, text, or messaging app, and Korea has one of the highest reported volumes of it in the world. What makes it different from a simple scam is the way Korean police and prosecutors treat the middle layer of the operation — the people who, often without fully realizing it, transfer funds, hand over a bank card, or receive money into their own account on someone else's instruction. Foreigners show up on both sides of this: as the person deceived out of savings, and as the person recruited, sometimes through a fake job posting, into moving stolen money and later facing criminal investigation for it.

→How the Scam Usually Works

Most voice phishing (보이스피싱) schemes follow a recognizable script, even as the specific pretext changes:

  • Impersonation of authority — a caller claims to be a prosecutor (검사), police officer, financial supervisory official, or bank employee, saying the victim's account is linked to a crime and must be "protected" by moving funds to a new "safe account"
  • Fake loan or refund offers — a message offers a low-interest loan (대출) or a tax refund, but processing it first requires paying a fee or verifying identity through an app that turns out to be malware
  • Romance or relationship scams — a long-running online relationship eventually asks for money for an emergency, customs fees, or an investment opportunity
  • Fake delivery or immigration notices — a text with a link claiming a package is stuck in customs or an immigration document needs urgent action, designed to install malware or harvest banking credentials

No legitimate prosecutor (검사), police officer, or bank employee in Korea will ever ask you to move money to a "safe account" over the phone — that instruction is, by itself, proof of a scam.

→If You Are the Victim: What to Do Immediately

  1. Call your bank immediately to request an account freeze (지급정지) on the receiving account — Korean banks can freeze a suspect account quickly if reported right after the transfer, and speed here matters more than almost anything else
  2. Report to the police (112) or the Financial Supervisory Service hotline (1332) and get a case number — this is required for both the freeze request and any later civil recovery
  3. File a formal victim report (피해신고) at a police station or online through the cyber investigation portal, with transaction records, call logs, and any messages preserved
  4. Apply for damage relief under the Special Act on Voice Phishing Damage Relief (통신사기피해환급법), which creates a process for recovering funds still sitting in the frozen receiving account before they're moved again

Recovery isn't automatic and isn't guaranteed — it depends heavily on whether the funds are frozen before they're withdrawn or forwarded again, which is why the first hour matters more than the first week.

→If You've Been Recruited as a Money Mule (Even Unknowingly)

This is the scenario foreign clients are most often blindsided by. A "job offer" — sometimes advertised as easy cash-handling or courier work — asks the person to receive money into their own bank account and forward it elsewhere, or to hand over their bank card (통장/카드) and PIN to a "manager" who promises a cut of the proceeds. Both are classic recruitment patterns for a money mule (대포통장 명의인, an account used under someone else's identity to launder scam proceeds), and both can result in the account holder being investigated for fraud (사기) or violation of the Act on the Prevention of Illegal Trading of Financial Accounts (전자금융거래법위반), regardless of whether they knew the money was stolen.

  • Korean courts do consider whether the person genuinely didn't know or had reason to suspect the scheme, but "I didn't know" is a defense that has to be proven with real facts, not simply asserted
  • Handing over a bank card and PIN to a stranger, even for a promised fee, is itself treated as a serious red flag by investigators, since it's a well-known recruitment pattern
  • A foreigner under investigation for this can also face collateral immigration consequences — a pending criminal case can complicate a visa renewal or extension even before any conviction
  • The account itself will typically be frozen and the holder listed on the Korea Federation of Banks' financial fraud offender registry (금융질서문란행위자), which can make it difficult to open new bank accounts in Korea for years afterward, independent of the criminal case outcome

→What Investigators Look At

FactorWhy It Matters
How the "job" was foundRecruitment through unofficial channels, messaging apps, or offers that seem too easy is treated as a warning sign the person should have recognized
What was actually handed overOnly receiving/forwarding money is treated differently than handing over the physical bank card and PIN
Communication with the recruiterMessages showing the person asked questions, hesitated, or was told to lie to the bank all factor into intent
Number and pattern of transactionsA single isolated transfer looks very different from repeated transactions structured to avoid the bank's fraud detection

→What to Do If You're Contacted by Police as a Suspect

  • Do not ignore a police summons (출석요구) assuming it will go away — nonresponse tends to make things worse, not better
  • Gather every message, job posting, and communication with whoever recruited you before the interview, not after
  • You have the right to remain silent and the right to an attorney during questioning — the same protections that apply in any Korean criminal investigation apply here
  • Get legal advice before the first police interview if at all possible; what you say in that first session shapes the rest of the case more than people expect

→Protecting Yourself Going Forward

  • Never move money on instructions from a caller claiming to be a prosecutor (검사), police officer, or bank official — hang up and call the institution back on its official number
  • Never hand over your bank card, account, or banking app credentials to anyone, no matter what the payment or job offer sounds like
  • Treat any "job" that involves receiving and forwarding money through your personal account as a scam recruitment attempt, not a legitimate opportunity
  • If you've already sent money or handed over a card, act within hours, not days — the freeze process (지급정지) depends on speed

Voice phishing (보이스피싱) cases move fast on both sides — the scam itself and the criminal exposure it can create for people who never intended to break the law. Whether you're trying to recover stolen funds or explain your side to investigators, the window to act well is short.
