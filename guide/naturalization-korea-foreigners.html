Naturalization (귀화) in Korea: How Foreign Residents Actually Become Korean Citizens

By Juan, Attorney at Law 8 min read English

Permanent residency (F-5) lets you stay in Korea indefinitely without a passport, a Korean passport, and the right to vote. Naturalization (귀화, gwihwa) does — and it is a different, slower, and far more demanding process than most long-term residents expect.

One of the most common misunderstandings I see from long-term foreign residents is treating permanent residency and citizenship as points on the same track, where enough years in Korea eventually convert one into the other automatically. They don't. F-5 permanent residency is an immigration status; naturalization (귀화) is a separate legal process under the Nationality Act (국적법) administered by the Ministry of Justice's nationality authorities, with its own eligibility rules, its own testing requirements, and its own timeline — one that can run well over a year from application to oath.

1The Three Paths to Naturalization

The Nationality Act (국적법) sets out three main naturalization tracks, and which one applies to you depends almost entirely on your personal circumstances rather than your choice:

  • General naturalization (일반귀화) — the standard track, requiring at least five consecutive years of lawful residence in Korea, along with the conduct, self-support, and other requirements described below
  • Simplified naturalization (간이귀화) — available to people with a qualifying family connection, most commonly a foreign spouse married to a Korean national. A spouse who has lived in Korea for two years while married, or who has been married for three years (with at least one year of Korean residence), can generally apply under a shortened residence requirement rather than the full five years
  • Special naturalization (특별귀화) — reserved for narrower categories: the biological children of Korean nationals, people who have rendered special merit to Korea, and certain individuals recognized for outstanding ability in science, the economy, culture, sports, or similar fields. This track can waive the residence requirement almost entirely, but qualifying is the exception, not the rule

Most applicants I work with fall into general naturalization (일반귀화) or, if married to a Korean national, simplified naturalization (간이귀화). Getting the track right at the outset matters — applying under the wrong category is one of the more common, and entirely avoidable, reasons an application stalls.

2The Residence, Conduct, and Self-Support Requirements

Regardless of track, the Nationality Act (국적법) imposes several substantive requirements beyond simple years of residence:

  • Legal capacity and age — the applicant must be an adult under Korean law at the time of application
  • Good conduct (품행 단정) — no disqualifying criminal record, and generally no history of serious immigration violations such as illegal stay or unauthorized employment
  • Sufficient assets or skills for self-support (생계유지능력) — evidence that the applicant can support themselves financially, shown through income records, property, or a stable job, rather than reliance on public assistance
  • Basic understanding of Korean language, customs, and values (기본소양) — assessed through the testing process described below

A clean criminal record and a demonstrable ability to support yourself are not formalities in a naturalization (귀화) application — they are substantive requirements the Ministry of Justice actually verifies, not just documents you attach and forget about.

3The Language and Culture Test

Most applicants satisfy the basic understanding (기본소양) requirement in one of two ways: completing the Korea Immigration and Integration Program (사회통합프로그램, commonly called KIIP) through its final stage, or passing a standalone comprehensive evaluation for naturalization (귀화용 종합평가) administered separately from KIIP. KIIP is the far more common route for residents who plan ahead, since it builds Korean language and society coursework into a structured program over several stages, culminating in an interview, rather than a single high-stakes exam. Applicants who skip KIIP and go straight for the standalone test tend to find it considerably harder to pass on the first attempt.

  • KIIP enrollment is open to most long-term visa holders well before they're eligible to actually apply for naturalization (귀화) — starting early is a genuine advantage, not just a formality
  • The final naturalization interview covers basic Korean language ability, knowledge of Korean law, history, and social norms, and sometimes questions specific to the applicant's own naturalization file
  • Some applicants — generally those with long-term marriage to a Korean national or advanced age — may qualify for reduced testing requirements, but this is assessed case by case

4Can You Keep Your Original Citizenship?

Korea's default rule under the Nationality Act (국적법) requires a naturalized citizen to renounce their prior citizenship — dual nationality is the exception, not the norm. That said, the exceptions are broader than many applicants assume:

CategoryDual Nationality Treatment
Marriage-based naturalization (혼인귀화)May retain original nationality if the applicant formally pledges not to exercise foreign nationality rights in Korea (외국국적불행사서약)
Special naturalization for exceptional talent (특별귀화)Often eligible for the same non-exercise pledge (외국국적불행사서약) rather than full renunciation
General naturalization (일반귀화)Generally requires proof of renunciation of the original nationality, submitted within a set period after approval
Overseas Koreans returning at an advanced ageA separate, narrower dual nationality allowance can apply depending on age and circumstances

If keeping your original citizenship matters to you, confirm which category you actually qualify under before applying — this is not a detail to sort out after approval, since the deadline to formally renounce or submit the pledge (외국국적불행사서약) runs on a fixed clock once naturalization is granted.

5The Application Process, Interview, and Oath

  1. File the application (귀화허가신청) with the required documents — identity records, criminal background checks from both Korea and your home country, proof of income or assets, and evidence supporting your chosen naturalization track
  2. Document and background review — immigration authorities verify residence history, criminal record, and financial documentation, which is often the slowest part of the process
  3. Interview — an in-person interview assessing Korean language ability, understanding of Korean society, and consistency with the documents filed
  4. Cabinet deliberation and presidential approval — naturalization in Korea is formally granted by presidential approval following Cabinet review, which is why the process, start to finish, commonly runs well over a year
  5. Oath and citizen registration — once approved, the new citizen takes an oath and completes registration, including renouncing prior nationality or submitting the non-exercise pledge (외국국적불행사서약) where applicable, within the required window

6What Gets Applications Denied

Naturalization (귀화) applications are discretionary, not automatic, even when an applicant technically meets the minimum requirements. Common reasons applications are denied or delayed include an undisclosed criminal record or immigration violation, gaps in the residence history that break the continuity requirement, insufficient or poorly documented proof of self-support, and inconsistencies surfacing at the interview stage that suggest the application wasn't prepared carefully. A denied application isn't necessarily the end of the road, but reapplying without addressing the underlying issue tends to produce the same result.

Naturalization (귀화) is the only process that actually makes you a Korean citizen — permanent residency, no matter how many years it's held, does not. For residents who intend to make Korea permanent in every sense, understanding which track applies, preparing the language and culture requirement early, and getting the dual nationality question right from the start makes the difference between a smooth application and one that drags on for years.
