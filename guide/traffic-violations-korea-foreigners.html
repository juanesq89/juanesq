Traffic Violations in Korea as a Foreigner: Fines, Points, and License Risk

By Juan, Attorney at Law 7 min read English

A speeding camera flash, an overstayed parking spot, a right turn on red you didn't know was illegal — in Korea, a small traffic violation (교통법규위반, gyotong beopgyu wiban) can quietly turn into a suspended license, a mounting fine, or a surprise at immigration.

Most foreigners driving in Korea assume traffic tickets work the way they do back home: pay the fine, move on. Korea's system is different in ways that catch people off guard — a points system (벌점, beoljeom) that stacks silently across separate violations, two entirely different types of "fines" with different legal consequences, and a license suspension (면허정지, myeonheo jeongji) or revocation (면허취소, myeonheo chwiso) process that can happen without a single day in court.

→Two Different Kinds of "Fine" — and They Are Not the Same

Korea's Road Traffic Act (도로교통법, doro gyotongbeop) splits traffic penalties into two tracks, and confusing them is the single most common mistake foreign drivers make.

A camera-issued administrative fine (과태료) is billed to whoever owns the car and carries no license points — it's closer to a parking ticket. An on-the-spot fine (범칙금) issued by a traffic officer who pulls you over is attached to you as the driver personally, and depending on the violation, adds points (벌점) to your license. Two speeding incidents that look identical from the driver's seat can have completely different consequences depending on which track they fall into.

→The Points System (벌점) Nobody Explains to Foreigners

Every officer-issued violation adds points (벌점) to your driving record. Accumulate enough within a set period and your license is suspended (면허정지) automatically — no hearing required.

  • 40 points in a single incident, or accumulated points reaching certain thresholds within 1–3 years, triggers automatic suspension
  • Common point values: speeding 20–40km/h over the limit (15 points), running a red light (15 points), using a phone while driving (15 points), driving without a seatbelt (10 points)
  • Repeated violations compound quickly — three unrelated tickets in one year can suspend a license that no single ticket would have touched
  • Serious violations (DUI, hit-and-run, certain repeat offenses) can trigger outright revocation (면허취소) rather than suspension

A suspended Korean license also suspends your ability to drive on an International Driving Permit here — the two are not independent.

→If You're Driving on a Foreign or International License

Many foreigners drive in Korea for a period on an International Driving Permit (국제운전면허증, gukje unjeon myeonheojeung) before converting to a Korean driver's license (한국 운전면허증). The Road Traffic Act (도로교통법) treats violations the same regardless of which document you're holding — points, fines, and suspension all apply. What changes is enforcement: a suspension or revocation tied to an International Driving Permit is recorded against you personally and can follow you into the Korean license conversion process later, sometimes blocking or delaying it.

→You Can Contest a Camera-Issued Fine

Most foreigners assume a camera photo is unchallengeable. It isn't. An administrative fine (과태료) can be formally objected to (이의신청, iui sincheong) within the notice period, typically by submitting a written objection to the issuing agency explaining why the violation shouldn't stand — a passenger was actually driving, the sign was obscured, an emergency situation applied, or the photo evidence is genuinely unclear. Objections are decided on the papers; there's rarely a hearing, so how the objection is written matters.

  1. Read the notice carefully. It states the deadline to object and the deadline to pay at a reduced early-payment rate.
  2. Gather evidence fast. Dashcam footage, photos of signage, or documentation of who was actually driving.
  3. File the objection (이의신청) in writing before the deadline — a late objection is not considered.
  4. If rejected, further appeal routes exist through an administrative court, though for small fines this is rarely worth the cost unless the underlying facts matter for something bigger, like a pending license suspension.

→Unpaid Fines Do Not Just Disappear

Ignoring a fine because you're leaving Korea soon is a common and costly mistake. Unpaid administrative fines (과태료) and on-the-spot fines (범칙금) escalate: late penalties accrue, the amount can be referred for compulsory collection, and unresolved traffic debt has in some cases been flagged during visa renewal or re-entry screening, particularly where multiple unpaid fines exist on the same foreign registration. It rarely blocks a routine renewal outright, but it is exactly the kind of loose thread immigration officers ask about — and exactly the kind of thing a returning foreigner doesn't want surfacing at the airport.

→What to Do If You've Been Cited

  • Identify which type of fine you received — camera-issued administrative fine (과태료) or officer-issued on-the-spot fine (범칙금) — the notice will state which
  • Check whether points (벌점) were assessed and how close you are to a suspension threshold
  • Pay before the early-payment deadline if you have no basis to contest — the discount is meaningful and the deadline is strict
  • If you have a genuine basis to dispute it, file the written objection (이의신청) before the deadline, not after
  • If you're facing suspension (면허정지) or revocation (면허취소), or a violation is tangled up with a visa issue, get advice before responding — the administrative and immigration consequences move on separate, overlapping clocks

Traffic law rarely feels urgent until the points add up or a renewal notice arrives with an unexplained hold on it. Understanding which system you're in — and which deadlines are actually enforced — is what keeps a minor violation from becoming a bigger problem.
